# Voice & Copy Guide
## Portfolio Site — Writing Rules

> Read this before writing any copy for this site.
> Every word on the site should feel like the same person wrote it.
> That person is direct, warm, specific, and never performs.

---

## The Core Principle

Occam's razor applied to writing.
The simplest version of a sentence is almost always the best version.
If a word can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.
If a sentence can be shorter without losing truth, shorten it.

---

## Voice in Four Words

**Direct.** Say the thing. No warm-up sentences. No "In this case study, I will..."
**Warm.** A human wrote this. Not a resume generator.
**Specific.** Name the constraint. Name the decision. Name what changed.
**Unhurried.** Short sentences. Room to breathe. Nothing crammed in.

---

## Sentence Rules

- Short sentences are almost always better than long ones
- One idea per sentence
- If a sentence needs a semicolon it probably needs to be two sentences
- Vary length — one long, two short, one medium — never uniform
- Start sentences with the subject when possible
- Active voice always. "I led the research" not "the research was led by me"
- Present tense where it makes sense. "I work at the intersection" not "I have worked"

---

## Punctuation Rules

- **No em dashes** — they are the single biggest AI tell. Use a period or comma instead.
- **No semicolons** in body copy. Split the sentence.
- **Periods over conjunctions.** "I did X. Then Y." not "I did X, and then I did Y."
- **Commas only when the sentence actually needs a pause.** Not decorative.
- **No ellipses** in professional copy.
- **No exclamation marks** anywhere on this site.
- **Contractions are fine** and make copy feel more human. "I don't" over "I do not."

---

## Word Rules

### Never use these words
These are AI tells or filler. Every single one:
- Delve, dive, explore
- Leverage, utilize (use "use")
- Seamless, robust, scalable
- Impactful, meaningful, powerful
- Innovative, cutting-edge, groundbreaking
- Passionate, driven, dedicated
- Journey (unless literal)
- Stakeholders (use "the team," "leadership," "engineering," whoever they actually are)
- Solutions (too vague — say what the solution actually was)
- Space (as in "the fintech space" — say "fintech")
- Deliverables (say what was actually delivered)
- Learnings (say "what I learned" or just state what you learned)
- Findings (say what you actually found)

### Always prefer the simpler word
- Use "show" not "demonstrate"
- Use "build" not "develop" or "create"
- Use "fix" not "address" or "resolve"
- Use "asked" not "interviewed" unless precision matters
- Use "made" not "produced" or "generated"
- Use "people" not "users" in body copy (users is fine in technical contexts)
- Use "I" not "the designer" when talking about your own work

---

## Copy Length Rules

**Hero headline:** 8 words maximum. One idea. No sub-clauses.
**Positioning statement:** 1 to 2 sentences. 25 words maximum total.
**About teaser (homepage):** 2 to 3 sentences. That is all.
**Case study section headlines:** Under 10 words. Plain language. No cleverness.
**Body paragraphs:** 2 to 4 sentences. Never more than 5.
**Stat labels:** 3 to 5 words maximum.
**Button copy:** 2 to 4 words. Specific action, not vague direction.
**Phase labels:** One word. Impact. Problem. Discovery. Solution.

## Specific Patterns to Avoid

### The setup sentence
Bad: "In order to understand the problem, I conducted user interviews."
Good: "I interviewed 8 people to understand the problem."

### The passive problem statement
Bad: "There was a need to improve the onboarding experience."
Good: "Onboarding was taking too long. Users were dropping off before they got to the core feature."

### The vague outcome
Bad: "The redesign improved the user experience significantly."
Good: "Task completion time dropped from 4 minutes to 90 seconds."

### The performed enthusiasm
Bad: "I'm passionate about creating products that make a difference."
Good: "I work on complex systems. Making them feel simple is the job."

### The responsibility list
Bad: "Responsibilities included leading design, collaborating with engineers, and presenting to stakeholders."
Good: "I led design end to end — research, decisions, and handoff. Engineering and product were in the room the whole way."

### The gerund opener
Bad: "Conducting research revealed that users were confused by the navigation."
Good: "The research was clear. Users couldn't find what they needed."

### The nominalization
Bad: "We had a discussion about the navigation structure."
Good: "We discussed the navigation structure."

## How to Write a Case Study Section

### Impact section
Lead with the result. State it in plain language.
Then the number or observation. Then a single sentence of context.
Never: "Through rigorous research and iterative design, we were able to achieve..."
Always: "Handle time dropped from 23 minutes to 14. That is a 39% reduction."

### Problem section
State what was hard. Then why it mattered.
Business goal in one sentence. User reality in one sentence.
Show the gap between them. That gap is the problem.

### Discovery section
What you did. What you found. What surprised you.
Three things. Not ten. The most important insight in the last sentence.

### Solution section
What you decided. What you rejected. Why.
One tradeoff per paragraph. No more.
End with something you would do differently. It shows maturity.

## Tone Calibration

This is a portfolio, not a pitch deck and not a journal entry.
The tone sits between a thoughtful portfolio review and a well-written product doc.
Professional without being cold. Human without being casual.

**Too cold:** "The user research phase involved 8 semi-structured interviews with operational staff to identify friction points in the claims workflow."
**Too casual:** "So basically I talked to a bunch of people and figured out what was annoying them."
**Right:** "I interviewed 8 people who used the claims system every day. They were frustrated, but not for the reason the analytics suggested."

---

## The Gut Check

Before finalizing any copy, read it out loud.
If you would not say it in a conversation, rewrite it.
If it sounds like it could have been written by anyone, it is not specific enough.
If it is longer than it needs to be, cut it.

One question for every sentence: does this sentence need to exist?
If the answer is not clearly yes, delete it.

## Reference Examples

### Positioning statement
Not this: "I am a passionate product designer who creates innovative user-centered experiences that drive business value across complex enterprise environments."
This: "I design products that turn complex systems into clarity. Seven years in fintech and enterprise SaaS."

### Case study opener
Not this: "This project involved redesigning the claims management workflow for a large financial services organization."
This: "40,000 people used this tool every day. Most of them hated it."

### About page
Not this: "I am a driven designer with a passion for solving complex problems through user-centered design methodologies."
This: "I work on hard problems. The kind where the business wants one thing and the people using it need something else. My job is to find the version that works for both."

### Impact stat label
Not this: "Percentage reduction in average task completion time"
This: "Faster to complete"

### Button copy
Not this: "Click here to view the full case study"
Not this: "Learn more"
This: "View case study"
